## The 100,000-Mile Question

A decade ago, 100,000 miles was considered the beginning of the end for most cars. Today, it's more like halftime. Modern engineering, synthetic oils, and better manufacturing have pushed vehicle lifespans well past the 200,000-mile mark — but that doesn't mean every car with 100K is a good buy.

Let's break down when a 100,000-mile car makes sense, when it doesn't, and how to tell the difference.

## The Financial Case For Buying at 100K Miles

### Massive Depreciation Savings

A car loses roughly 60% of its value in the first 100,000 miles. A Toyota Camry that cost $30,000 new might sell for $12,000–$15,000 at 100K. That means someone else absorbed $15,000–$18,000 in depreciation.

If the car has 150,000 miles of life left, you're buying that remaining life at a fraction of the original cost. The math is compelling.

### Lower Insurance Costs

High-mileage cars have lower values, which means lower comprehensive and collision insurance premiums. Depending on the car, you might save $500–$1,000 per year on insurance.

### Possible to Buy Outright

At $8,000–$15,000, many 100K-mile cars are affordable enough to buy without financing. No car payment means no interest and lower monthly costs overall.

## The Risks of Buying at 100K Miles

### Major Services Coming Due

Around 100,000 miles, several expensive maintenance items come due:

- **Timing belt/chain service:** $500–$1,200
- **Transmission fluid change:** $150–$300
- **Spark plugs:** $200–$400
- **Water pump:** $400–$800
- **Suspension components:** $500–$1,500

If the previous owner skipped these, you're inheriting their deferred maintenance. Budget $1,000–$3,000 for catch-up maintenance in the first year.

### Increased Repair Frequency

Cars with 100K+ miles simply need more repairs. Components wear out: alternators, starters, AC compressors, power steering pumps. None of these are catastrophic individually, but they add up.

### Unknown History Risk

By 100,000 miles, a car has likely had 2-3 owners. Each ownership transfer is an opportunity for maintenance to lapse. Without clear records, you're making assumptions about the car's care.

## Which Cars Are Worth Buying at 100K Miles?

### Great Buys at 100K

- **Toyota Camry/Corolla** — These are practically mid-life at 100K
- **Honda Civic/Accord** — Proven to go 250K+ with maintenance
- **Toyota Tacoma/4Runner** — Built for extreme mileage
- **Lexus ES/RX** — Toyota reliability with luxury features
- **Mazda3/CX-5** — Excellent long-term track records

### Risky Buys at 100K

- **Any European luxury car** — Maintenance costs escalate dramatically
- **Nissan CVT models** — Transmission failures common at this mileage
- **Turbo engines without records** — Turbos need documented maintenance
- **Any car with no maintenance history** — The biggest risk factor

## The 5 Things to Check on Any 100K-Mile Car

### 1. Maintenance Records

This is the single most important factor. A car with complete dealer records at 100K is infinitely more trustworthy than one with no records at 50K. Ask for every receipt, and walk away if they can't provide any.

### 2. Transmission Health

Feel for smooth shifts in automatics. Any hesitation, hard shifting, or slipping means the transmission is on borrowed time. A transmission replacement costs $3,000–$6,000.

### 3. Engine Compression

A compression test ($50–$100 at a mechanic) tells you the true health of the engine. Even, strong compression across all cylinders means the engine has plenty of life left.

### 4. Suspension Condition

Worn suspension is normal at 100K, but it needs to be budgeted for. Bounce each corner of the car — it should settle in one bounce. Multiple bounces mean worn shocks/struts ($800–$1,500 to replace all four).

### 5. Rust Inspection

Rust is the silent killer of otherwise healthy cars. Inspect the undercarriage, wheel wells, and rocker panels. Surface rust is manageable; structural rust is a death sentence.

## The Math: Should You Buy?

Here's a simple framework:

**Good deal if:**
- Purchase price + expected 2-year maintenance < price of equivalent lower-mileage car
- The car has documented maintenance history
- It's a model known for longevity
- No major mechanical issues found in inspection

**Bad deal if:**
- Major services were skipped and need immediate attention
- The car has structural rust or accident history
- It's a model with known 100K-mile failure points
- No maintenance records exist
